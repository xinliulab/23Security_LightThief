LightThief · USENIX Security 2023 · MATLAB artifact

Reading the code through the paper's signal model.

LightThief steals optical-wireless (OWC) data by letting the room's data-coded light switch a passive tag that backscatters an ambient RF carrier through the wall. This page is a reading map for the end_to_end MATLAB artifact: it follows the exact signal chain of the paper and, at every stage, names the file that implements it, the variables that carry the signal, and the figure that verifies it.

Input: LightThief Recovered: LightThief BER 0 at the demo operating point

Six signals, kept distinct

The most important thing to get right in this system is which signal is which. The paper's mechanism only makes sense if these are not conflated.

Three facts the model must respect. (1) The optical signal is itself data-coded — Manchester-coded On-Off-Keying — not an unmodulated carrier. (2) The tag's binary switching control is that same light: the light's On/Off directly sets the photodiode impedance, so the coded optical signal is the reflection-control square wave (no logic circuit). (3) The reflected RF gets its harmonics from that optical square wave itself (paper Eq. 7–8) — there is no separate sub-carrier — and both the light and the RF are three-dimensional waves that spread as 1/(4πd²).
Signal 1

Coded optical signal

Manchester-OOK light intensity, a square wave at the optical clock rate fo. Stage 3.

Signal 2

Switching control

The same light On/Off, now read as the tag's 0/1 reflect / no-reflect command. Stage 4.

Signal 3

Reflected RF

Ambient carrier gated by the light: r(t)=b(t)·sin(2πfct). Stage 5–6.

Signal 4

Propagation

3-D Friis spreading through the wall sets the received strength / SNR. Stage 7.

Signal 5

Received signal

Down-converted m=1 harmonic plus CFO, timing drift, and AWGN. Stage 7.

Signal 6

Sync & decode

BPSK symbols → bits → frame → Hamming → recovered text. Stage 8–9.

The reflection in two equations

The data-coded light is a square wave; switching the reflection with it multiplies the ambient continuous wave (CW) by that square wave. The square wave's odd harmonics translate the data onto clean sidebands.

Ssqw(fo, θn) = 0.5 + (2/π) Σm=1,3,5… (1/m)·cos(2π·m·fo·t + θn) paper Eq. 7 The optical Manchester square wave as a Fourier series. The bit sets the phase θn ∈ {0, π} (Manchester ≡ BPSK). The 0.5 term is the carrier that survives at fc.
Sr = Scw × Ssqw = (1/π) Σm odd (1/m)·{ sin(2π(fc+m·fo)t + θn) + sin(2π(fc−m·fo)t − θn) } paper Eq. 8 Reflected RF = CW × square wave. The OWC data θn rides on harmonics at fc ± m·fo, away from the CW — this is the self-interference avoidance. The receiver (Sec. 5.3) down-converts the first harmonic fc+fo and recovers θn.

The pipeline, stage by stage

Each stage lists what the paper specifies, the signal and variables the code produces, the MATLAB files that implement it, and the generated figure that verifies it. Start at run_demo.m (the conductor) and lt_params.m (all parameters); regenerate every figure below with make_figures.

01
Paper Sec. 6 — OWC transmitter

Coded bitstream

What the paper says

The OWC driver turns text into 8-bit ASCII, then a 12-bit Hamming(12,8) code plus one parity bit (a 13-bit codeword), and prepends a 10-bit preamble 1111000010 for packet detection.

Signal & variables

A bit vector frame_bits = preamble + 13-bit codewords. Parameters p.preamble, p.code_len.

MATLAB files

build_frame.mencode_text.mencode_byte.mhamming_12_8.mlt_params.m

02
Paper Sec. 3.2, Fig. 4 — line coding

Manchester chips

What the paper says

OWC encodes OOK with Manchester code so the light is 50% balanced (no flicker). Each bit becomes two chips: 1→[1 0], 0→[0 1]. Crucially, "Manchester code is essentially BPSK".

Signal & variables

chips = manchester_enc(frame_bits), two chips per bit.

MATLAB files

manchester_enc.moptical_waveform.m

03
Paper Sec. 3.2 — intensity modulation · SIGNAL 1

Optical OOK light waveform

What the paper says

The chips drive the LED's light intensity by On-Off Keying. The result is a square wave at the optical clock rate fo — the actual transmitted, data-bearing optical signal.

Signal & variables

light = held square wave b(t) ∈ {dark, bright}; p.fo is the optical clock rate (= bit rate).

MATLAB files

optical_waveform.m

04
Paper Sec. 4.1, Fig. 5 — passive tag · SIGNAL 2

Tag switching control

What the paper says

Incident light changes the photodiode impedance, which switches the antenna between a reflecting (short) and non-reflecting (matched) state — with no logic circuit or energy harvester. The light On/Off is the 0/1 switching command.

Signal & variables

The same light b(t) now read as the reflection state Γ(t). Signals 1 and 2 are one physical waveform.

MATLAB files

backscatter_reflect.m (calls optical_waveform.m)

05
Paper Sec. 4.4, Eq. 8 — backscatter · SIGNAL 3

Reflected RF waveform

What the paper says

The tag reflects the ambient continuous wave only while the light is on, so the reflected RF is the CW multiplied by the optical square wave: Sr = Scw × Ssqw (Eq. 8).

Signal & variables

r = light .* sin(2*pi*p.fc*t) — the carrier is gated by b(t). p.fc, p.fs_rf.

MATLAB files

backscatter_reflect.m

06
Paper Sec. 4.4, Fig. 10 — frequency shift

Reflected spectrum / harmonic comb

What the paper says

The square wave's odd Fourier harmonics place copies of the data at fc ± m·fo (m = 1,3,5…), separated from the CW. This frequency shift is what avoids self-interference; the data lives in each harmonic's phase θn.

Signal & variables

FFT of r: a strong carrier line at fc and data lobes at fc ± m·fo (1/m falloff).

MATLAB files

backscatter_reflect.m + FFT in run_demo.m / make_figures.m

07
Paper Eq. 1, 4 & Sec. 5.3 — propagation · SIGNALS 4–5

3-D propagation & reception

What the paper says

The light (Eq. 4) and the reflected RF (Eq. 1, Friis) are three-dimensional waves spreading as 1/(4πd²) over distances dph, dt, dr; this link budget sets the received strength. The attacker then down-converts the first harmonic fc+fo, removes DC, and low-pass filters (Sec. 5.3).

Signal & variables

propagation(p, geom) → link.snr_db; band_select(r,p,1) → baseband bb; channel_apply adds CFO/phase/timing/AWGN → rx.

MATLAB files

propagation.mband_select.mchannel_apply.mlt_default_channel.m

08
Paper Sec. 5.3 — BPSK demodulation

Synchronization

What the paper says

Recover the symbol clock with timing recovery, then estimate and correct phase and amplitude for symbol detection. Selecting the harmonic and integrating over a bit period is the matched filter that demodulates the Manchester/BPSK.

Signal & variables

Integrate-and-dump matched filter → coarse CFO → timing phase → static de-rotation → Costas loop → synced BPSK symbols (one per bit).

MATLAB files

recover.mmatched_filter.mcoarse_cfo.msymbol_sync.mestimate_timing_phase.mderotate_phase.mcostas_bpsk.m

09
Paper Sec. 5.3, 6 — frame parse & FEC

Decode the data

What the paper says

Cross-correlate with the preamble for frame synchronization, then parse the frame and recover the OWC data: undo the codeword with Hamming correction and the parity check.

Signal & variables

Preamble correlation gives frame start + resolves the BPSK 0/π ambiguity; each 13-bit codeword → hamming_decode → byte. res.text, res.bytes.

MATLAB files

decode_bits.mfind_frame_start.mhamming_decode.mber_calc.m

10
Paper Sec. 7.2, Fig. 16a — evaluation

End-to-end performance

What the paper says

The attack is judged by bit error rate over the recovered packets — the paper reports BER vs. data rate through real walls (Fig. 16a).

Signal & variables

A BER vs. Eb/N0 sweep over the full coding chain under CFO, phase, timing drift, and AWGN.

MATLAB files

run_ber.mmake_figures.m
